DNA : Contains A and G (purines), C and T (pyrimidines), arranged in a double helix of two strands held together by hydrogen bonds (A with T, and C with G)

RNA : Contains A and G (purines), C and U (pyrimidines), arranged in a single strand

  • mRNA : Messenger RNA; carries genetic information from DNA to the ribosome for protein synthesis
  • tRNA : Transfer RNA; carries amino acids to the ribosome during protein synthesis
  • rRNA : Ribosomal RNA; forms the core of the ribosome and catalyzes protein synthesis

DNA Replication : Occurs during S-Phase of Cell Cycle, goes from 5' to 3' direction. Helicase unzips the double strand, DNA polymerase adds onto the nucleotides, Proofreading enzymes minimize errors during the process.

Frameshift Mutation : Deletion or insertion of nucleotides that shifts the reading frame of the genetic code

Missense Mutation : Substitution of wrong nucleotide into DNA

Nonsense Mutation : Substitution of a wrong nucleotide that results in a stop codon

Transcription : The process of synthesizing RNA from DNA

RNA Processing : Introns are spliced out and exons are joined together: 3' poly-tail and 5' cap

Translation : Process which mRNA is specific squence of amino acid is lined up on ribosome for protein synthesis

Codon : Three nucleotides that specify a particular amino acid in the protein sequence

Intron : Non-coding DNA sequences that are removed during RNA processing

Exon : Coding DNA sequences that are retained in the mature mRNA

Promoter : A DNA sequence where RNA polymerase binds to initiate transcription

Repressor : A protein that binds to the operator region and prevents transcription

Inducer : Molecule that binds to inactivate repressors

Operator : Short sequence near promoter that assist in transcription by interacting with transcription factors

Operon : A inducer that only allows production of genes when needed

Viruses : Parasitic infectious agents that require a host cell to reproduce. Can contain both DNA and RNA, or have a viral envelope (protective coating)

  • Lytic Cycle : Virus is actively reproducing and kills the host cell
  • Lysogenic Cycle : The virus lies dormant within the host cell's DNA

Retrovirus : RNA virus that uses reverse transcriptase to convert its RNA genome into DNA

Prion : A neurodegenerative pathogen that causes misfolding of normal proteins into misfolded pathogenic forms

Viroids : tiny plant viruses

Phage : A virus that infects bacteria

Bacteria : Prokaryotic cells; consist of one double-stranded circular DNA molecule; reproduce by binary fission

Plasmid : Small, circular DNA molecules found in bacteria; can replicate independently of the chromosomal DNA

Genetic Recombination

Transformation : When a bacteria take up foreign DNA from the environment and incorporate it into their own genetics

Transduction : Movement of genetics from one bacteria to another via a virus which causes crossover

  • Generalized : Lytic cycle accidently places DNA into a phage which in brought into another cell
  • Specialized : Virus that leaves lysogenic cycle and brings host DNA into a phage

Conjugation : Transfer of genetic material between bacteria connected by sex pili

Genetic Engineering

Restriction Enzymes : Enzyme that cuts DNA at a particular sequence which creates a sticky end (single short strand of DNA from 3' to 5')

Vector : A DNA molecule used to artifically transfer genetic material into another cell where it can be replicated of expressed

Cloning : A slow process by which a desired DNA sequence is copied multiple times

Gel Electrophoresis : A technique used to separate DNA by size

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) : A biotechnology technique used to create millions of copies of a specific DNA sequence

Four Types of Evolution

Genetic Drift : Change in allele frequencies due to random, unpredictable events from one generation to another

Gene Flow : Genes move from one population to another due to migration of individuals or gametes

Mutation : Random and permanent changes in DNA sequence that serves as the primary source of genetic variation

Natural Selection : An organism's ability to survive and reproduce in a given environment

Requirements for Natural Selection

Variation : Differences in traits among individuals in a population

Heritability : Trait must be able to be passed from parents to offspring

Differential Reproductive Success : Variation in parents on how muchh offspring they produce can result in differnet traits

Selection Types

Directional : When environmental pressure favors one extreme phenotype/genotype over another which shifts the traits on a population in that direction

Stabilizing : Favors average phenotypes/genotypes and selects against extreme variations which reduces genetic diversity and stablizes the population

Disruptive (Diversifying) : Favors the extreme phenotypes/genotypes at both ends of the distribution rather than the middle

Sexual : Favors individuals with traits that increase their chances of attracting mates and reproducing

Artifical : Human-controlled selection for desired traits

Sources of Variation within Population

Mutation : Random changed in DNA can introduce new alleles into a population

Sexual Reproduction : Combines genetic material from two parents, creating unique combinations of alleles

Balanced Polymorphism : Maintenance of two or more phenotypes at a stable frequency in a population due to natural selection

Speciation (Process by which species evolve)

Allopatric Speciation : Interbreeding stops due to a physical barrier and populations diverges into two or more. If the new population evolves separately and changes, then they cannot reproduce with each other and speciation have occured

Sympatric Speciation : Interbreeding stops without physical barriers preventing it

  • Polyploidy : When a individual have a higher number of chromosome sets, they cannot reproduce with nonpolyploid individuals

  • Balanced Polymorphism : When phenotypic traits become too different that the two groups stop interbreeding

Other Terms

Adaptation : When a population becomes better suited to its environment through the accumulation of favorable genetic variants

Adaptive Radiation : The process by which a single ancestral species evolves into multiple descendant species, each adapted to a specific ecological niche

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium : p + q = 1, p2 + 2pq + q2. The rules for this are no mutations, no gene flow, no genetic drift, no natural selection and random mating

Homologous Character : Traits similar between organisms that came from the same ancestor

Vestigial Character : Traits that have lost their original function through evolution

Gradualism : The idea that evolutionary changes occur slowly and steadily over time

Punctuated Equilibria : The theory that evolutionary changes occur in rapid bursts followed by long periods of stability

Heterotroph Theory : The theory that early organisms obtained energy by consuming other organisms

Convergent Character : Traits that similar between two or more organisms that don't share a common ancestor; parallel evolution

Convergent Evolution : Two unrelated species evolve similar traits due to similar environmental pressures

Divergent Evolution : The process by which related organisms evolve different traits due to different environmental pressures

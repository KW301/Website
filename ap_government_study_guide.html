Unit 1

Foundations of American Democracy

~15–22% of exam
Core Philosophical Foundations
Natural Rights (John Locke)
The idea that people are born with rights that no government can legitimately take away. Locke identified them as life, liberty, and property. Jefferson adapted this in the Declaration as "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness." The key implication: if government violates these rights, people have the right to overthrow it.
★ FRQ link → Declaration of Independence, social contract, justification for revolution
Social Contract
The theory (Locke, Rousseau) that people voluntarily give up some freedoms to a government in exchange for protection of their remaining rights. Critical point: the government only has legitimate authority as long as it upholds its end — protecting the people. If it fails, the people may dissolve it. This is the philosophical basis for the Declaration of Independence.
★ Always connect this to popular sovereignty and limited government
Popular Sovereignty
The principle that all governmental authority comes from the people. The government governs only with the consent of the governed. In the U.S., this is expressed through elections, representation, and the amendment process. It's why "We the People" opens the Constitution.
Limited Government
Government power is restricted by law — the Constitution defines what government can and cannot do. No official is above the law. This is operationalized through separation of powers, checks and balances, and the Bill of Rights. Opposite of arbitrary/tyrannical government.
Articles of Confederation (1777) — Why It Failed
The U.S.'s first government. It was deliberately weak because the founders feared a strong central government (they had just escaped one). Fatal weaknesses: Congress could not tax (had to beg states), could not regulate interstate/foreign commerce, no executive to enforce laws, no federal judiciary, each state had one vote regardless of size, required unanimous consent of all 13 states to amend, and could not draft soldiers. Shays' Rebellion (1786–87) — an armed uprising of Massachusetts farmers — exposed how powerless the federal government was to maintain order. This convinced leaders a new framework was needed.
★ Know all the weaknesses — the Constitution was designed to fix each one
Constitutional Convention Compromises (1787)
Great Compromise: solved the representation fight between large vs. small states. Created a bicameral legislature — House based on population (favors large states), Senate equal (2 per state, favors small states).
Three-Fifths Compromise: enslaved people counted as 3/5 of a person for apportionment (representation) and taxation — gave Southern states more House seats and Electoral College votes.
Commerce/Slave Trade Compromise: Congress couldn't ban slave trade until 1808.
Electoral College: indirect election of president — a compromise between direct popular vote and congressional election.
★ Understand WHAT problem each compromise solved
Federalism — The Big One
Types of Federalism & Power Distribution
FederalismThe division of power between a national government and state governments. Neither level can abolish the other. This was a major innovation — before the U.S., countries typically had either fully centralized or fully decentralized governments. The Constitution lists specific national powers and reserves remaining powers to states (10th Amendment).
Enumerated PowersPowers explicitly listed for Congress in Article I, Section 8. Includes taxing, borrowing money, coining currency, declaring war, regulating interstate/foreign commerce, establishing post offices, raising an army/navy, and creating courts. These are the only powers Congress is expressly given.
Implied PowersPowers not explicitly listed but logically derived from enumerated powers via the Necessary & Proper (Elastic) Clause. Example: Congress can charter a national bank because it has the power to manage currency — even though "bank" isn't mentioned. McCulloch v. Maryland established this broadly.
Necessary & Proper ClauseArticle I §8 — Congress can "make all laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into execution" its enumerated powers. Called the Elastic Clause because it stretches federal power. Anti-Federalists (Brutus No. 1) feared this would make federal power unlimited.
Reserved PowersThe 10th Amendment states powers not given to the federal government are kept by the states or the people. These are called "police powers" — the broad authority to regulate health, safety, welfare, and morals. Examples: marriage law, speed limits, education, criminal law.
Concurrent PowersPowers shared by both federal and state governments. Most importantly: taxing, borrowing money, establishing courts, and making/enforcing laws. Both levels of government can tax the same income, for instance.
Supremacy ClauseArticle VI — the Constitution and federal laws made under it are "the supreme Law of the Land." When federal and state law conflict, federal law wins. This prevents states from nullifying federal laws. McCulloch v. Maryland used this to strike down Maryland's tax on the federal bank.
Full Faith & CreditArticle IV — states must respect each other's public acts and legal decisions (court rulings, contracts, licenses). A marriage legal in one state must be recognized in all states.
Dual Federalism ("Layer Cake")The historical model (pre-New Deal, ~1789–1937) where federal and state powers were clearly separated — each had its own distinct domain with little overlap. The national government handled its enumerated powers; states handled everything else. Like two separate cake layers that don't mix.
Cooperative Federalism ("Marble Cake")The modern model (post-New Deal, 1930s onward) where federal and state governments work together to solve problems, with their powers intermingled. Federal grants fund state programs. Example: Medicaid is a federal program administered by states with federal money and federal rules. Like marble cake — the flavors are swirled together.
Categorical GrantsFederal money given to states for a specific, defined purpose with conditions and strings attached. States must follow federal rules to receive the money. Example: federal highway money that requires states to maintain a certain speed limit. Gives Congress more control over state policy.
Block GrantsFederal money given to states for broad policy areas with fewer strings attached. States have more flexibility in how they spend it. Example: TANF (welfare) money. Favored by conservatives because they give more autonomy to states. States prefer block grants; federal government tends to prefer categorical grants (more control).
Unfunded MandatesFederal requirements imposed on states without providing the money to pay for compliance. Example: The Clean Air Act sets air quality standards states must meet, but Congress doesn't always fund enforcement. States hate these because they impose costs without resources. The Unfunded Mandates Reform Act (1995) tried to limit them.
DevolutionThe political effort to shift power and responsibility from the federal government to states. Championed by conservatives and Republicans (Reagan's "New Federalism," Clinton's welfare reform). The idea: states are "laboratories of democracy" that can tailor solutions to local needs.
Interstate CompactsFormal agreements between two or more states on a shared issue, requiring congressional approval. Example: the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey, water-sharing agreements, the Multistate Tax Compact.
Checks & Balances
Congress → Executive
  • Override presidential veto (2/3 both chambers)
  • Senate confirms appointments (cabinet, judges)
  • Senate ratifies treaties (2/3 vote)
  • Impeach and remove president
  • Controls the budget; can defund programs
  • War Powers Act (60-day troop limit)
Executive → Congress
  • Veto legislation (president can reject any bill)
  • Pocket veto (if Congress adjourns)
  • Recommend legislation (SOTU address)
  • Call Congress into special session
  • Executive orders (bypass Congress)
  • Going public / bully pulpit pressure
Judicial → Both
  • Judicial review — strike down laws (Marbury)
  • Strike down executive orders as unconstitutional
  • Interpret what laws actually mean
  • Life tenure creates independence from politics
  • Unanimous or majority opinions set precedent
Unit 2

Interactions Among Branches of Government

~25–36% of exam — BIGGEST UNIT
Congress
House of Representatives
Size & Terms435 members, 2-year terms. Because ALL members are up for reelection every 2 years, House members are highly responsive to public opinion — they're always in campaign mode.
Speaker of the HouseMost powerful person in Congress. Controls the legislative agenda, appoints committee chairs, refers bills to committees. Chosen by the majority party.
Rules CommitteeThe "traffic cop" of the House — sets the rules for floor debate on each bill (how long it can be debated, what amendments are allowed). Called the most powerful committee because it controls what bills actually get voted on.
Revenue BillsAll bills raising taxes must originate in the House (Article I, §7). This was designed so the chamber closest to the people (2-year terms) controlled the power to tax.
Discharge PetitionIf 218 members (a majority) sign a petition, a bill can be forced out of committee and onto the floor for a vote — bypassing the committee chair who is blocking it. Rarely successful.
Senate
Size & Terms100 members (2 per state), 6-year staggered terms. Only 1/3 up for election every 2 years — makes the Senate more stable and insulated from short-term political swings. Represents states equally.
FilibusterA Senate tactic where a senator (or minority party) refuses to stop talking, preventing a vote from happening. Can last for days. Used to block legislation the minority strongly opposes. The threat alone can kill a bill.
ClotureThe Senate vote to end a filibuster. Requires 60 votes (a "supermajority"). If the majority party has fewer than 60 seats, it needs bipartisan support to pass anything significant — this is why 60 is the magic number in the Senate.
Advice & ConsentSenate's constitutional role to confirm or reject presidential nominations (cabinet, federal judges, Supreme Court justices) and ratify treaties. Treaties need 2/3 Senate approval. Major check on executive power.
Unanimous ConsentSenate agreements where all 100 senators agree to speed up procedures (skip certain votes, limit debate time). Because any one senator can object, this requires cooperation.
Legislative Vocabulary (Full Explanations)
BicameralismHaving two separate legislative chambers — the House and Senate — each with different structures, powers, and constituencies. A bill must pass both chambers in identical form before going to the president. Designed to slow legislation and require broader consensus.
Standing CommitteeA permanent committee in Congress with jurisdiction over a specific policy area (Foreign Relations, Armed Services, Judiciary, Appropriations, etc.). Bills are assigned to the relevant committee, where they are debated, amended, or killed. Most legislation dies in committee — never reaching the floor.
Conference CommitteeA temporary joint committee of House and Senate members formed to resolve differences when the two chambers pass different versions of the same bill. They produce a compromise version that both chambers must vote on again.
MarkupThe committee process of reviewing and amending a bill line-by-line before voting to send it to the floor. This is where the real legislative work happens — the public floor debate is often performative.
GerrymanderingDrawing congressional district boundaries to give one party a political advantage. "Packing" concentrates opposition voters into a few districts (so they win big but win few seats). "Cracking" splits opposition voters across many districts (so they never have a majority anywhere). Named after Governor Elbridge Gerry, whose 1812 district looked like a salamander.
Incumbency AdvantageThe significant political edge that sitting members of Congress have over challengers. They have name recognition, can use their franking privilege (free mail), have staff to help constituents, have raised money before, can claim credit for federal projects in the district, and have access to media. House incumbents win reelection ~90% of the time.
Pork Barrel / EarmarksFederal spending directed to a specific member's district to benefit constituents and make the member look good. Also called "earmarks." Example: a federal grant for a new highway in a swing district. Criticized as wasteful; defended as democratic (representatives serving their people).
LogrollingTrading votes between legislators — "I'll vote for your bill if you vote for mine." Allows each member to get something for their district by supporting others' priorities. Common in budget and appropriations bills.
OversightCongress's power to monitor and investigate the executive branch to ensure laws are faithfully executed and agencies are functioning properly. Conducted through committee hearings, subpoenas, audits, and the Government Accountability Office (GAO). Example: Senate hearings on executive agencies or investigations like Watergate.
ImpeachmentThe constitutional process to remove federal officials. The House votes to impeach by simple majority (charges = "articles of impeachment"). The Senate holds the trial and needs a 2/3 vote to convict and remove. The Chief Justice presides in presidential impeachment trials. Presidents Andrew Johnson, Bill Clinton, and Donald Trump (twice) were impeached; none were convicted by the Senate.
Mandatory SpendingSpending required by existing law that Congress does not vote on annually — it happens automatically. Includes Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and interest on the national debt. Makes up ~65% of the federal budget and grows automatically as the programs grow.
Discretionary SpendingSpending that Congress must actively appropriate each year through the budget process. Includes defense, education, transportation, and most agency budgets. About 30% of the federal budget. This is where Congress debates "how much to spend."
Franking PrivilegeMembers of Congress can mail official communications to constituents for free. Helps incumbents stay in touch with voters and reinforces the incumbency advantage — challengers must pay postage.
The Executive Branch
Formal (Constitutional) Presidential Powers
VetoPresident can reject a bill passed by Congress. Congress can override with 2/3 vote in both chambers. Presidents use the veto threat as leverage to influence legislation before it passes.
Pocket VetoIf Congress adjourns within 10 days of sending a bill to the president, and he doesn't sign it, the bill dies automatically — Congress is not available to override. Cannot be overridden.
Commander-in-ChiefPresident is the top military commander. Can deploy forces, but the War Powers Act (1973) requires notification of Congress within 48 hours and withdrawal within 60 days unless Congress authorizes the conflict.
Treaty PowerPresident negotiates treaties with foreign governments, but they must be ratified by 2/3 of the Senate. Because this is a high bar, presidents often use executive agreements instead.
Appointment PowerPresident nominates cabinet members, federal judges, Supreme Court justices, ambassadors, and agency heads. All require Senate confirmation (majority vote). Recess appointments can bypass Senate confirmation temporarily.
Pardon PowerPresident can pardon individuals for federal crimes. Absolute for federal offenses; cannot be overridden. Cannot pardon for state crimes or impeachments. Has been controversial (Ford's pardon of Nixon).
Informal (Extra-Constitutional) Presidential Powers
Executive OrdersPresidential directives to the executive branch that have the force of law without needing a congressional vote. Examples: Truman's desegregation of the military, Obama's DACA, Trump's travel ban. Can be overturned by courts, revoked by successor presidents, or defunded by Congress. Important: they only apply within the executive branch.
Executive AgreementsInternational agreements with foreign leaders made without Senate ratification. Functionally like treaties but don't require the 2/3 Senate vote. Can be revoked by the next president. Used increasingly more than formal treaties.
Executive PrivilegeThe claimed right of the president to keep White House communications confidential from Congress and courts. Not in the Constitution — recognized by SCOTUS in U.S. v. Nixon (1974) but held to be limited (Nixon had to hand over tapes). Rooted in separation of powers and need for candid executive advice.
Signing StatementsWritten documents issued when a president signs a bill into law, stating how the president interprets the law or which provisions he considers unconstitutional and won't enforce. Criticized as allowing presidents to effectively veto parts of a law without a formal veto.
Bully PulpitThe president's unique platform to influence public opinion — speeches, press conferences, social media, State of the Union. Presidents "go public" to build pressure on Congress by rallying citizens. Term coined by Teddy Roosevelt.
War Powers Act (1973)Congress's attempt to reassert control over military deployment. Requires president to notify Congress within 48 hours of deploying troops and withdraw them within 60 days (plus 30-day withdrawal) unless Congress authorizes continued operations. Most presidents have argued it's unconstitutional, and it has rarely been enforced.
Bureaucracy
Federal BureaucracyThe massive set of agencies, departments, and offices in the executive branch that implement and administer federal law. Over 2 million civilian employees. Includes cabinet departments (State, Defense, Treasury, etc.), independent agencies (EPA, FDA, FEC), and government corporations (USPS, Amtrak).
Bureaucratic DiscretionThe authority agencies have to fill in the details Congress leaves undefined when writing laws. Congress often writes laws in general terms ("protect the environment"), and agencies decide what that means in practice through regulations. This is where much of the real policymaking happens.
Rule-MakingThe bureaucratic process of creating regulations — essentially writing specific rules to implement a law. Published in the Federal Register; open to public comment. Regulations have the force of law.
Merit System / Civil ServiceFederal employees are hired based on qualifications and competitive exams, not political connections. The Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act (1883) created this after the spoils system (patronage) led to corruption and the assassination of President Garfield.
Iron TriangleA stable, mutually beneficial relationship between three actors: a congressional committee, a federal agency, and an interest group in the same policy area. Example: the Armed Services Committee + Pentagon + defense contractors. Each benefits the others: Congress funds the agency and the contractors support the committee members' reelection; the agency implements policies that benefit contractors; contractors lobby Congress to support the agency. Very hard to disrupt.
Issue NetworkA broader, more fluid web of policy actors — including think tanks, academics, media, and advocacy groups — who all care about a policy area but don't have the stable, closed relationships of an iron triangle. Modern policymaking is often characterized by these looser networks.
Principal-Agent ProblemThe challenge that Congress (the "principal") faces in controlling the bureaucracy (the "agent"). Once an agency is created, it develops its own goals, expertise, and relationships — making it hard for Congress to control what it actually does. Agencies may prioritize their own survival over congressional intent.
The Judicial Branch
Judicial ReviewThe power of federal courts to review laws and executive actions and strike down those that violate the Constitution. Not explicitly in the Constitution — established by Chief Justice John Marshall in Marbury v. Madison (1803). This makes the Supreme Court the final arbiter of what the Constitution means. It's why SCOTUS is powerful despite having no army or budget.
Original JurisdictionThe authority to hear a case for the first time (not on appeal). The Supreme Court has original jurisdiction in cases involving disputes between states and cases involving foreign ambassadors. Most cases reach SCOTUS through appellate jurisdiction.
Appellate JurisdictionThe authority to review decisions made by lower courts. This is how ~99% of Supreme Court cases arrive. Parties who lose in lower courts petition SCOTUS to review the decision.
Certiorari ("Rule of Four")Certiorari ("cert") is the formal request for SCOTUS to hear a case. At least four of the nine justices must agree to grant cert. SCOTUS receives ~7,000–8,000 petitions per year and hears about 60–80 cases. By deciding not to hear a case, SCOTUS lets the lower court ruling stand.
Stare DecisisLatin for "let the decision stand." The principle that courts should follow prior rulings (precedents) when facing similar cases. Provides stability and predictability. When SCOTUS overrules a precedent (like overturning Roe v. Wade in 2022), it's a major legal event.
Judicial ActivismA philosophy where judges use their power broadly to advance rights or correct injustices, even if it means departing from precedent or the text's literal meaning. Examples: Brown v. Board, Obergefell v. Hodges. Often associated (though not exclusively) with liberal courts.
Judicial RestraintA philosophy where judges defer to the elected branches and only strike down laws when there's a clear constitutional violation. Judges should follow the text's original meaning and avoid "legislating from the bench." Associated with conservative/originalist justices.
Amicus CuriaeLatin for "friend of the court." Third-party briefs submitted by interest groups, governments, or experts who have a stake in the case's outcome but aren't a party to it. Example: the ACLU submitting a brief in a civil liberties case. Shows how interest groups use the courts as a political strategy.
Opinion TypesMajority opinion: binding precedent; the official ruling of the Court. Concurring opinion: agrees with the majority ruling but for different reasons. Dissenting opinion: disagrees with the majority — sometimes later become the basis for overturning old precedents.
Senatorial CourtesyThe tradition that the president will consult with senators from the nominee's home state before appointing federal district judges. Senators from the president's party effectively have veto power over nominations in their state.
Unit 3

Civil Liberties & Civil Rights

~13–18% of exam
Civil Liberties
Protections FROM the government
Things the government cannot do to you. Based on the Bill of Rights. Example: the government can't search your house without a warrant or stop you from speaking your mind.
Civil Rights
The right to equal treatment
The government must treat everyone equally. Based on the 14th Amendment. Example: the government cannot discriminate against you based on race or sex.
Key Terms — Civil Liberties
Incorporation DoctrineOriginally the Bill of Rights only limited the federal government. The 14th Amendment changed that — courts used it to make the Bill of Rights apply to state governments too. Each right was applied state-by-state through court cases. Today almost every right in the Bill of Rights applies to all 50 states.
Establishment ClauseFrom the 1st Amendment. The government cannot create an official religion, prefer one religion over another, or favor religion over no religion. That's why public schools can't lead prayers (Engel v. Vitale). Think of it as a wall between church and state.
Free Exercise ClauseAlso from the 1st Amendment. The government cannot stop you from practicing your religion. Your beliefs are always protected — but actions tied to religion can sometimes be regulated. Example: Amish kids were allowed to stop school after 8th grade (Wisconsin v. Yoder) because their faith required it.
Prior RestraintWhen the government tries to block something from being published before it comes out. This is almost always unconstitutional. The government can punish you after you publish something harmful, but it generally can't stop you beforehand. Example: Nixon couldn't stop newspapers from printing the Pentagon Papers.
Symbolic SpeechActions that send a message — protected as free speech even though no words are spoken. Examples: wearing armbands to protest a war (Tinker v. Des Moines), burning a flag. The government can't ban it just because people find it offensive.
Clear & Present Danger → Imminent Lawless ActionTwo standards for when speech loses protection. The old test (Schenck, 1919): speech creating a "clear and present danger" could be restricted. The current stricter test (Brandenburg, 1969): only speech that directly incites imminent lawless action can be restricted. This means almost all speech — even hateful or extreme speech — is protected unless it's about to cause immediate violence.
Exclusionary RuleIf police find evidence by violating your 4th Amendment rights (illegal search, no warrant), that evidence cannot be used against you in court. It gets "excluded." Why? To discourage police from breaking the rules. Case: Mapp v. Ohio (1961).
Miranda RightsBefore police question someone in custody, they must read them their rights: you have the right to remain silent, anything you say can be used against you, you have the right to a lawyer. If they don't do this, statements made during questioning can be thrown out. Based on Miranda v. Arizona (1966).
Right to PrivacyNot written anywhere in the Constitution, but courts have found it implied by several amendments (1st, 3rd, 4th, 9th). Protects personal decisions from government interference — like a married couple's right to use contraception (Griswold v. Connecticut, 1965). Was the basis for abortion rights in Roe v. Wade, which was overturned in 2022 (Dobbs).
Due Process (Procedural)The government must follow fair procedures before taking away your life, liberty, or property. You get notice, a hearing, a chance to respond. Think of it as "the government has to play by the rules."
Due Process (Substantive)Some rights are so fundamental that the government can't take them away no matter what procedures it follows. Even a perfectly fair process can't justify violating them. This is how courts protect rights not explicitly listed (like privacy and marriage).
Key Terms — Civil Rights
Equal Protection Clause14th Amendment — states cannot deny anyone equal protection of the laws. This is the main tool for challenging laws that discriminate. Courts apply different levels of scrutiny depending on who is being discriminated against.
Strict ScrutinyThe toughest legal test. Applied when a law discriminates based on race, national origin, or a fundamental right. The government must prove: (1) there's a compelling reason for the law and (2) the law is as narrow as possible. Most laws that face this test get struck down.
Intermediate ScrutinyThe middle test. Applied to laws that discriminate based on sex or gender. The government must show the law is substantially related to an important goal. Harder than rational basis, easier than strict scrutiny.
Rational Basis TestThe easiest test. Applied to most ordinary laws. The government just needs any reasonable justification — almost any explanation works. Very few laws fail this test.
Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)The bad precedent. SCOTUS said "separate but equal" was constitutional — racial segregation was fine as long as facilities were technically equal. This justified Jim Crow laws for 60 years.
Brown v. Board (1954)Overturned Plessy. Court said separate is inherently unequal — segregated schools violate the Equal Protection Clause. One of the most important decisions ever. Sparked the civil rights movement.
Civil Rights Act (1964)Made it illegal to discriminate based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin in public places (restaurants, hotels) and employment. Finally gave federal law teeth to enforce equal treatment.
Voting Rights Act (1965)Banned the tricks Southern states used to stop Black voters — literacy tests, grandfather clauses, intimidation. Required states with bad voting rights records to get federal approval before changing voting laws (preclearance).
Affirmative ActionPolicies that try to increase representation of historically excluded groups in schools or jobs. Courts have said race can be one factor in college admissions (Bakke), but racial quotas are illegal. In 2023 (Students for Fair Admissions), SCOTUS struck down race-conscious admissions at Harvard and UNC.
De Jure vs. De Facto SegregationDe jure = segregation by law (Jim Crow — literally written into statutes). De facto = segregation in practice without a law requiring it (like racially divided neighborhoods from economic patterns). Brown fixed de jure; de facto is much harder to address legally.
Unit 4

American Political Ideologies & Beliefs

~10–15% of exam
How People Form Political Beliefs
Political SocializationThe process through which people develop their political views over their lifetime. It starts in childhood and continues through adulthood. Your political identity comes from where you grew up, who raised you, what you've experienced — not from thin air.
FamilyThe biggest single influence on political identity. Children tend to adopt the same party as their parents. If you grew up in a Democrat household, you're statistically likely to be a Democrat. This is why party identification tends to be stable across generations.
SchoolsTeach civic values — respect for democracy, national identity, how government works. Things like the Pledge of Allegiance, civics class, and mock elections promote participation in the political system. Schools tend to build legitimacy for the system more than push a specific ideology.
MediaShapes what issues people care about and how they see them. The rise of partisan media (Fox News, MSNBC) and social media reinforces existing views — people mostly consume content that agrees with them ("confirmation bias"). This contributes to political polarization.
Generational EffectsBig historical events shape the views of entire generations. The Great Depression created loyal Democrats. Vietnam shaped Boomer distrust of government. Gen Z came of age during extreme polarization and COVID — tends to be more progressive on social issues.
Gender GapWomen are more likely to identify as Democrats; men more likely Republican. This gap has existed since 1980 and has grown over time. One of the most reliable predictors of voting behavior. Women tend to favor social spending, gun control, and healthcare more than men on average.
ReligionEvangelical Christians are strongly Republican. Black Protestants and most Jewish voters are strongly Democratic. Religion shapes views on abortion, LGBTQ+ rights, and social policy.
Political Ideologies — Plain English
Liberal / Progressive
  • Want government to reduce inequality and protect rights
  • OK with higher taxes on wealthy to fund programs
  • Support abortion rights, LGBTQ+ rights, immigration reform
  • Favor gun control, environmental regulations, social safety nets
  • Tend to vote Democrat
Conservative
  • Want less government, especially in the economy
  • Free markets, lower taxes, less regulation
  • Generally oppose abortion, support traditional values
  • Strong national defense; stricter immigration enforcement
  • Tend to vote Republican
Libertarian
  • Want minimal government in ALL areas — social AND economic
  • Free markets AND personal freedom — don't restrict either
  • Legalize drugs, anti-surveillance, anti-war, anti-regulation
  • Neither traditionally liberal nor conservative
Moderate
  • Mix of liberal and conservative views depending on the issue
  • Most likely to be a swing voter
  • Largest, most diverse group — hard to predict
  • Often decide close elections
Public Opinion & Polling
Public OpinionWhat the general public thinks about a political issue. It's not just one person's view — it's the aggregate of millions of people's attitudes. Politicians track it constantly to know what voters care about.
Random SamplingThe key to accurate polling. Every person in the population must have an equal chance of being selected. A random sample of 1,000 people can accurately represent 330 million Americans — it's not about size, it's about randomness. Non-random samples (like only calling landlines) produce biased results.
Margin of ErrorThe built-in uncertainty in any poll. If a poll says 52% support Candidate A with a ±3% margin of error, the real number is somewhere between 49% and 55%. A smaller sample = larger margin of error. Always check this when reading polls.
Push PollA fake poll used as a disguised political attack. The "questions" are really designed to make you think negatively about a candidate: "Would you be more or less likely to vote for X if you knew he raised taxes on veterans?" Not a real poll — a manipulation tool.
Political CultureThe shared values that almost all Americans agree on regardless of party — things like belief in democracy, individual rights, rule of law, and free speech. Different from public opinion (which changes often) — political culture is stable and deeply held.
Salient IssuesThe issues voters care most about right now. Changes over time. The economy is almost always salient. During a pandemic, healthcare becomes salient. Politicians try to make their strongest issues salient — if you're strong on defense, keep defense in the news.
Unit 5

Political Participation

~20–27% of exam
Elections & Voting
Electoral CollegeHow we actually elect the president. Each state gets electors equal to its total seats in Congress (House + Senate). There are 538 total electors; a candidate needs 270 to win. Almost every state is winner-take-all — whoever wins the state's popular vote gets ALL its electors. This is why candidates focus on swing states (Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, Arizona) instead of campaigning everywhere equally.
Primary vs. General ElectionPrimary: party members vote for who will represent their party. General: all voters choose between the parties' nominees. Primaries attract highly engaged (often more extreme) voters, which pushes candidates toward their party's base — then they try to move to the center for the general.
Open vs. Closed PrimaryClosed: only registered party members can vote. Open: any registered voter can vote in either party's primary. Open primaries tend to produce more moderate nominees because more centrist voters participate.
Voter TurnoutPercentage of eligible voters who actually vote. Presidential elections: ~55–66%. Midterms: ~40–50%. Local: often under 20%. Turnout is higher among: older voters, higher education, higher income, and white voters (historically). Young and low-income voters turn out less — this shapes who politicians actually listen to.
Incumbency AdvantageCurrent officeholders win reelection at very high rates (~90% in the House). Why: name recognition, easier fundraising, free mail (franking), media access, credit-claiming for local projects, and gerrymandering. This is why Congress is so hard to change even when people are unhappy with it.
Midterm ElectionsCongressional elections held 2 years into a presidential term. The president's party almost always loses seats. Voters use midterms to express frustration. Big examples: Democrats lost 63 House seats in 2010; Republicans lost 40 in 2018.
Coattail EffectA popular presidential candidate helps their party's congressional candidates win in the same election. Voters vote the whole ticket. Works in reverse too — unpopular presidents drag their party down.
Motor Voter Act (1993)Required states to let people register to vote at the DMV and other government offices. Made registration much easier — especially for lower-income Americans who might not know where to register otherwise. Increased registration rolls significantly.
Voter ID LawsState laws requiring photo ID to vote. Supporters say it prevents fraud; opponents say it suppresses turnout among minorities, elderly, and low-income people who are less likely to have qualifying ID. Courts have debated whether these effectively impose an unconstitutional poll tax.
GerrymanderingDrawing district lines to give one party an advantage. Packing: cram opponents into one district so they win big but win few. Cracking: split opponents across many districts so they never have a majority. Courts have limited racial gerrymandering but generally allow partisan gerrymandering.
Political Parties
Two-Party SystemThe U.S. basically only has two major parties. This is caused by winner-take-all elections — if you come in second, you get nothing, so voters consolidate into two large coalitions rather than splitting into many smaller parties. Most other democracies use proportional representation, which produces multiple parties.
Party FunctionsParties recruit candidates, raise money, set the policy agenda, mobilize voters, and organize government. They're the main vehicle for turning voter preferences into actual policy. Without parties, it would be very hard to coordinate millions of voters around shared goals.
Third PartiesThird parties almost never win significant office in the U.S. But they can act as spoilers — pulling enough votes from a major party to cost it the election. Example: Ralph Nader in 2000 (Florida). They also introduce new ideas that major parties later adopt.
Party RealignmentA major shift in which groups support which party, lasting for decades. Example: the South was solidly Democratic from the Civil War through the 1960s, then shifted to Republican after the Civil Rights Act. These shifts reshape the entire political landscape.
PluralismTheory that many different groups compete for power and no single group dominates. The government acts as a referee. Policy results from compromise between competing interests. This is the optimistic view of interest group politics.
ElitismTheory that a small wealthy elite actually controls government policy regardless of what ordinary voters want. The wealthy use donations, lobbying, and connections to get their preferred policies regardless of elections.
HyperpluralismCritique that too many competing interest groups create gridlock. Every group can block what it hates but none is strong enough to pass what the public needs. Government becomes paralyzed trying to please everyone.
Interest Groups
Interest GroupsOrganizations of people who share a goal and work to influence government policy in their favor. They're different from political parties — they don't run candidates, they just try to influence whoever wins. Types: business groups (Chamber of Commerce), labor unions (AFL-CIO), single-issue groups (NRA, Sierra Club), professional groups (AMA).
LobbyingDirectly contacting and trying to persuade government officials to support your position. Professional lobbyists are often former members of Congress or agency officials who use their connections and knowledge of the system. Protected by the 1st Amendment (right to petition).
PACPolitical Action Committee. Raises money and donates directly to candidates' campaigns. Regulated — can only donate up to $5,000 per candidate per election. Must disclose donors. Created by companies, unions, and issue groups.
Super PACCreated after Citizens United (2010). Can raise and spend unlimited money on political advertising as long as it doesn't directly coordinate with a campaign. Must disclose donors. Billionaires can effectively fund entire political ad campaigns through these.
Dark MoneyPolitical spending by nonprofits that don't have to disclose their donors. They run political ads but the public never finds out who paid for them. Critics say it lets wealthy interests secretly buy political influence.
Citizens United v. FEC (2010)SCOTUS ruled corporations and unions have 1st Amendment free speech rights and can spend unlimited money on political advertising. Led directly to the explosion of Super PACs. One of the most controversial decisions in recent history — dissent argued money isn't speech and this drowns out ordinary voters.
Free Rider ProblemPeople benefit from an interest group's work without joining or paying dues. If a union wins higher wages for everyone in an industry, non-members get the raise too — so why join? Groups solve this by offering benefits only members get (newsletters, insurance, discounts).
Grassroots LobbyingInstead of hiring lobbyists, groups mobilize ordinary citizens to contact their representatives directly. Example: the NRA getting millions of members to call Congress. Effective because elected officials care about constituent pressure more than anything else.
Mass Media
Agenda SettingMedia controls what issues people pay attention to. By covering some topics constantly and ignoring others, media shapes what the public thinks is important. Politicians try to get media to focus on their strongest issues. "The media doesn't tell you what to think — it tells you what to think about."
FramingMedia controls how you think about an issue — what angle or context is used. Same facts, completely different impression: "Government welfare spending" vs. "Safety net for struggling families" vs. "Taxpayer-funded handouts." Frames shift opinion without changing underlying facts.
PrimingMedia influences what standards you use to judge politicians. If the news is all about the economy, you'll judge the president on the economy. Politicians try to "prime" voters to evaluate them on their strengths.
GatekeepingTraditional media decides what stories are worth covering. Editors and producers are "gatekeepers" who filter what becomes news. Social media has weakened gatekeeping — anyone can publish anything, which increases both information access and misinformation.
Horse Race JournalismMedia obsessing over who's winning or losing in polls rather than covering what candidates actually believe. More focus on gaffes, fundraising, and strategy than on policy substance. Critics say it trivializes democracy.
DisintermediationPoliticians now communicate directly with voters via social media, cutting out traditional media as the middleman. Trump's Twitter use is the clearest example. Allows unfiltered communication — but no fact-checking or editorial judgment either.
Required

Foundational Documents — What Each One Argues

For the FRQ: Don't just know the document exists. Know the argument it makes and what AP topic it connects to. You'll be asked to use these as evidence in FRQ 4 (Argument Essay).
Declaration of Independence · Jefferson, 1776
Main argument: People have natural rights (life, liberty, pursuit of happiness) that no government can take away. Government only gets its power from the people. If a government consistently violates people's rights, the people have the right to overthrow it. Lists grievances against King George to show Britain had violated this contract.
Simple version: We have rights by nature. Government exists to protect them. If it doesn't — we can replace it.
AP LINK → Natural rights, social contract, popular sovereignty, limited government
Articles of Confederation · 1777
Main argument: Created a very weak central government on purpose — the founders were scared of tyranny. Congress couldn't tax, couldn't regulate trade, couldn't enforce laws. Every state had one vote. Changing it required all 13 states to agree.
Simple version: America's first government was too weak to work. Its failure is the reason the Constitution was written.
AP LINK → Why federalism was redesigned; what problems the Constitution fixes
Federalist No. 10 · Madison
Main argument: Factions (groups that push their own interests at the expense of others) are inevitable in a free society — you can't stop them without destroying freedom. The solution is a large republic: (1) so many factions that none can dominate, (2) representatives filter and improve raw public opinion, (3) size makes it harder for any single group to seize control.
Simple version: People will always form self-interested groups. A big representative republic controls this better than a small direct democracy.
AP LINK → Factions, pluralism, why large republic is better than direct democracy
Federalist No. 51 · Madison
Main argument: Because people aren't angels, government needs to be designed to control itself. Give each branch the tools to fight back against the others — "ambition must counteract ambition." Separation of powers + checks and balances + federalism = a system where power stays limited because everyone is watching everyone else.
Simple version: People are self-interested. Structure government so each part keeps the others in check.
AP LINK → Checks and balances, separation of powers, why government is structured this way
Federalist No. 70 · Hamilton
Main argument: We need a single, strong executive — not a committee. One president means: fast decisions in a crisis, clear accountability (you know who to blame), and the ability to act with energy. A committee executive would be slow, secretive, and no one would be responsible for anything.
Simple version: One president is better than a committee because of speed, secrecy in foreign affairs, and accountability.
AP LINK → Why there's one president, executive power, presidential accountability
Federalist No. 78 · Hamilton
Main argument: The judicial branch is the "least dangerous branch" — it has no army and no money, so it's naturally the weakest. To protect it from political pressure, judges need lifetime tenure. Courts also need judicial review — the power to strike down unconstitutional laws — because the Constitution is the people's supreme will and must be protected even from elected majorities.
Simple version: Courts are the weakest branch and need independence (life tenure) to do their job of protecting the Constitution.
AP LINK → Judicial review, judicial independence, life tenure, least dangerous branch
Brutus No. 1 · Anti-Federalist, 1787
Main argument: The Constitution gives the federal government too much power. The Necessary & Proper Clause + the Supremacy Clause together mean Congress can pass almost any law it wants and override state law. The federal courts will just keep expanding federal power over time with no check. A large republic can't protect people's rights — only small, local governments close to the people can do that.
Simple version: The Constitution makes the federal government too powerful. States and individual rights will lose. This is WHY we got the Bill of Rights — to address these concerns.
AP LINK → Anti-Federalist critique, limits of federal power, why Bill of Rights was added
Letter from Birmingham Jail · MLK, 1963
Main argument: Written to white clergymen who told MLK to slow down and be patient. Key points: (1) There is never a convenient time to fight for justice — "justice too long delayed is justice denied." (2) People have a moral duty to disobey unjust laws — laws that degrade human dignity are not real laws. (3) Nonviolent direct action is necessary to force negotiations. (4) The white moderate who prefers "order" over justice is a bigger obstacle than the KKK.
Simple version: Waiting for change is itself a form of injustice. Peaceful protest and civil disobedience are morally required when the law itself is wrong.
AP LINK → Civil disobedience, civil rights movement, moral vs. legal law, role of protest
Democracy in America · Tocqueville, 1835
Main argument: A French visitor's observations on what makes American democracy work. Key insights: (1) Americans form voluntary associations (clubs, groups, civic orgs) constantly — this distributes power and keeps any one group from dominating. (2) The big danger in democracy is "tyranny of the majority" — the majority can oppress minorities just as badly as any tyrant. (3) Religion gives Americans shared moral values that make democracy function.
Simple version: American democracy works because of civic associations and shared values — but majority opinion can be just as tyrannical as a king.
AP LINK → Interest groups, voluntary associations, majority tyranny, civil society
SCOTUS

15 Required Cases — Plain English

For FRQ 3: Know each case's (1) constitutional provision, (2) what the court decided, (3) why it matters. You'll compare one of these to a new case you've never seen before.
Marbury v. Madison 1803
Power: Judicial Review
John Adams appointed judges on his last night as president. Madison (Jefferson's Secretary of State) refused to deliver the commissions. Marbury sued.

The Court said: Marbury deserved the job, but SCOTUS didn't have the authority to order it under the Judiciary Act — because that part of the Act was unconstitutional. In ruling that law unconstitutional, Marshall established that courts have the power to strike down laws that violate the Constitution. This power (judicial review) is now the foundation of everything the Supreme Court does.
→ Why: Federalist 78 predicted this; courts now have real power despite having no army
McCulloch v. Maryland 1819
Power: Necessary & Proper Clause + Supremacy Clause
Maryland tried to tax the federal bank. Two questions: (1) Can Congress create a bank? (2) Can a state tax it?

Marshall said: (1) Yes — the Necessary & Proper Clause gives Congress implied powers beyond what's listed. Creating a bank is a reasonable way to manage money. (2) No — "the power to tax is the power to destroy." States can't use their powers to undermine federal institutions. The Supremacy Clause settles it: federal law wins.
→ Why: Confirmed that the federal government is more powerful than Anti-Federalists wanted
United States v. Lopez 1995
Power: Commerce Clause Limits
Congress passed a law making it a federal crime to carry a gun near a school. The Court struck it down.

Reason: Carrying a gun near a school isn't an "economic activity" that substantially affects interstate commerce. Congress can't use the Commerce Clause to regulate anything it wants — there have to be actual limits. States retain power over local, non-economic activity. First time in 60 years the Court limited Commerce Clause power.
→ Why: Protects federalism — federal power has actual limits; 10th Amendment still means something
Engel v. Vitale 1962
1st Amendment: Establishment Clause
A New York school district composed a short, nondenominational prayer and had students recite it each morning. Parents sued.

The Court said: Doesn't matter that it's nondenominational or voluntary. The government writing and promoting an official prayer is an establishment of religion — full stop. Schools are especially sensitive because kids feel pressure to conform. No government-composed prayer in public schools.
→ Why: Separation of church and state; school religious activities must be student-initiated, not government-led
Wisconsin v. Yoder 1972
1st Amendment: Free Exercise Clause
Wisconsin required school attendance until age 16. Amish families withdrew their kids after 8th grade because high school education conflicted with their way of life and faith.

Court said: The Amish community's sincere religious beliefs outweigh the state's interest in compulsory schooling here. Free Exercise rights can sometimes override neutral state laws when the religious burden is substantial and the state's interest isn't compelling enough. Amish kids could stop at 8th grade.
→ Why: Religion can sometimes beat neutral laws; government must balance its interests against sincere religious practice
Tinker v. Des Moines 1969
1st Amendment: Student Speech / Symbolic Speech
Students wore black armbands to protest the Vietnam War. The school suspended them.

Court: "Students do not shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate." Wearing armbands is symbolic speech — a 1st Amendment-protected form of expression. Schools can only restrict speech that causes a substantial disruption to the educational process. Being uncomfortable or unpopular doesn't count. Students won.
→ Why: Students have real constitutional rights; schools can't suppress speech they just disagree with
New York Times v. United States 1971
1st Amendment: Freedom of Press / Prior Restraint
The Pentagon Papers revealed the government had lied about Vietnam. The Nixon administration tried to stop newspapers from publishing them.

Court: The government did not meet the very heavy burden needed to justify prior restraint (censorship before publication). Embarrassing the government or even harming its diplomatic efforts doesn't meet the bar. Papers could publish. Political secrets are not the same as imminent national security threats.
→ Why: Prior restraint is almost never OK; press freedom is a fundamental check on government power
Schenck v. United States 1919
1st Amendment: Limits on Speech in Wartime
Schenck distributed pamphlets urging men to resist the WWI draft.

Court upheld his conviction and established the "clear and present danger" test: speech can be restricted if it creates a clear and present danger of bringing about a harm Congress can prevent. Famous analogy: falsely shouting "fire!" in a crowded theater isn't protected.

⚠️ This test was later weakened by Brandenburg v. Ohio (1969), which requires imminent lawless action.
→ Why: Historical "clear and present danger" standard — still tested on AP even though the modern standard is stricter. Know it alongside Brandenburg v. Ohio (not a required case, but often taught alongside Schenck to show how speech protection evolved)
Shaw v. Reno 1993
14th Amendment: Racial Gerrymandering
North Carolina drew a congressional district stretching 160 miles that was majority-Black — designed to create a Black-majority district and elect a Black representative. White voters sued arguing it was unconstitutional racial gerrymandering.

Court: Race cannot be the predominant factor in drawing district lines. When a district is drawn so irregularly that it can only be explained by race, it is subject to strict scrutiny and is presumptively unconstitutional. Race-based gerrymandering — even when intended to help a minority group — violates the Equal Protection Clause. States CAN consider race as one factor, but not the primary driver.
→ Why: Limits racial gerrymandering; equal protection applies even when the intent is to help minorities gain representation
Gideon v. Wainwright 1963
6th Amendment: Right to an Attorney
Gideon was charged with a felony in Florida. Florida only gave free lawyers in capital (death penalty) cases. He represented himself and lost.

Court: The 6th Amendment right to an attorney is so fundamental that states must provide one to any defendant who can't afford one in a felony case. Without a lawyer, a fair trial is basically impossible. This forced states to create public defender systems nationwide.
→ Why: Incorporation of 6th Amendment; public defenders exist because of this case
Griswold, Roe & Dobbs 1965 / 1973 / 2022
14th Amendment: Right to Privacy — Topic 3.9 Content (NOT FRQ 3 cases)
These three cases form a required trio for Topic 3.9 (Right to Privacy). Know all three together — they tell a single story.

Griswold v. Connecticut (1965): Court struck down a law banning contraception for married couples. Established the right to privacy — found in the "penumbras" of the 1st, 3rd, 4th, 5th, and 9th Amendments. Government can't intrude into the intimate decisions of married life.

Roe v. Wade (1973): Extended Griswold's privacy right to cover abortion decisions. Struck down state abortion bans. Divided pregnancy into trimesters to determine when states could regulate. Grounded in 14th Amendment substantive due process.

Dobbs v. Jackson (2022): Overturned Roe. Held there is no constitutional right to abortion — it's not in the text and wasn't historically recognized. Returned the issue entirely to state legislatures. Most states now have different laws.
→ ⚠️ College Board update: These appear on MC and FRQ 4 (essay) as required content, but are NOT used in FRQ 3 (SCOTUS comparison). Know the privacy arc: Griswold → Roe → Dobbs
McDonald v. City of Chicago 2010
2nd Amendment: Incorporation to States
Chicago had a handgun ban. The Court said this violated the 2nd Amendment.

Building on DC v. Heller (2008) — which confirmed the 2nd Amendment protects an individual right to own firearms — McDonald incorporated the 2nd Amendment to the states via the 14th Amendment. States and cities cannot ban handguns. The right isn't unlimited — some regulations are still OK — but total bans are not.
→ Why: 2nd Amendment now applies to state and local governments, not just the federal government
Brown v. Board of Education 1954
14th Amendment: Equal Protection / Desegregation
Black students were required to attend separate schools from white students. The NAACP sued.

Unanimous Court: Racial segregation in public schools is inherently unequal and violates the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment. Overturn Plessy v. Ferguson (1896). Even if the buildings were identical, segregation itself sends a message of inferiority that harms Black children's development. Sparked the civil rights movement.
→ Why: Equal protection, overturning Plessy, civil rights movement launched; most important 14th Amendment case
Citizens United v. FEC 2010
1st Amendment: Campaign Finance / Corporate Speech
A nonprofit made a film criticizing Hillary Clinton during the 2008 primary. FEC said they couldn't air it under campaign finance law.

Court: Corporations and unions have 1st Amendment free speech rights. The government cannot limit how much they spend on political advertising (independent expenditures). Led directly to Super PACs. Opponents: money isn't speech, this lets the wealthy drown out everyone else. Supporters: free speech applies to all, including organizations.
→ Why: Super PACs exist because of this; unlimited outside spending in elections; most controversial recent case
Baker v. Carr 1962
Equal Protection: Reapportionment / "One Person, One Vote"
Tennessee hadn't redrawn its legislative districts in 60 years, so rural areas had far more representation per person than cities. Courts had refused to touch this as a "political question."

Court: Reapportionment IS a constitutional question courts can decide. This opened the door to Reynolds v. Sims (1964): "one person, one vote" — districts must be roughly equal in population. Ended massive rural over-representation in state legislatures.
→ Why: Courts can hear redistricting cases; equal vote means equally-sized districts

Constitutional Amendments — Exam Priority Guide

Your teacher is right. You do NOT need to memorize all 27 equally. The exam heavily tests specific amendments tied to Unit 3 (civil liberties/rights), Unit 1 (federalism), and Unit 5 (voting). Low-priority ones are here for completeness but rarely appear.
🔴 Must Know Cold — Appear Constantly on the Exam
#NameWhat It Does + Why It's Tested
1FRAPPSFreedom of Religion, Speech, Press, Assembly, Petition. The most-tested amendment by far. Covers: Establishment Clause (no state religion — Engel v. Vitale), Free Exercise Clause (can't ban religious practice — Yoder), free speech (Tinker, Schenck), free press (NY Times v. US), and symbolic speech. Almost every civil liberties question touches this.
2Right to Bear ArmsIndividual right to own firearms (DC v. Heller, 2008). States and cities can't ban handguns (McDonald v. Chicago, 2010). The right is not unlimited. Tested via McDonald — the incorporation of the 2nd Amendment — and in civil liberties questions about individual rights vs. public safety.
4Search & SeizurePolice need a warrant based on probable cause to search you or your property. The exclusionary rule (Mapp v. Ohio) enforces this — illegally obtained evidence can't be used in court. Appears in civil liberties questions about rights of the accused and police power.
5Self-Incrimination & Due ProcessRight to remain silent (can't be forced to testify against yourself — "pleading the 5th"). Due process required before the government takes your life, liberty, or property. Also covers double jeopardy and just compensation (eminent domain). Miranda rights are based here.
6Right to Counsel & Fair TrialSpeedy and public trial, impartial jury, right to know charges, right to confront witnesses, right to a lawyer. If you can't afford one, the government must provide one (Gideon v. Wainwright). Heavily tested through the Gideon required case.
10Reserved PowersPowers not given to the federal government belong to the states or the people. The anchor of federalism. Tested constantly in federalism questions — states keep "police powers" (health, safety, welfare). U.S. v. Lopez used this to limit Congress. Connects to Brutus No. 1 arguments.
14Equal Protection + Due Process + CitizenshipThe single most litigated amendment. Equal Protection Clause: states must treat people equally — basis for Brown v. Board, civil rights law, affirmative action, strict/intermediate/rational basis scrutiny. Due Process Clause: how the Bill of Rights gets applied to states (incorporation doctrine). Citizenship: birthright citizenship. Comes up in almost every Unit 3 question.
🟡 Know Well — Tested Regularly, Especially in Context
8Cruel & Unusual PunishmentNo excessive bail, excessive fines, or cruel and unusual punishment. Death penalty is constitutional but not for juveniles or intellectually disabled people. Appears in civil liberties questions about rights of the accused.
9Unenumerated RightsRights not listed in the Constitution still exist — the people keep them. Foundation (with the 14th) for the right to privacy. Appears in questions about Griswold v. Connecticut and substantive due process.
13Abolition of SlaveryAbolished slavery and involuntary servitude (except as punishment for crime). 1865. One of the three Reconstruction Amendments (13, 14, 15). Tested in civil rights and historical context questions.
15Voting Rights — RaceCan't deny the vote based on race. 1870. Despite this, Black voters were systematically excluded through Jim Crow tactics until the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Tested in voting rights and civil rights movement questions.
17Direct Election of SenatorsSenators elected by popular vote (not state legislatures). 1913. Tested in federalism questions — changed how states are represented in Congress and made the Senate more democratically accountable.
19Women's SuffrageCan't deny the vote based on sex. 1920. Tested in voting rights, political participation, and civil rights questions. Connects to Seneca Falls Declaration (required document).
22Presidential Term LimitsMax two presidential terms. Passed after FDR's four terms. Tested in executive branch questions about presidential power and the two-term norm.
24No Poll TaxCan't charge a fee to vote in federal elections. 1964. Tested in voting rights questions — poll taxes were used to suppress Black voter turnout in the South.
25Presidential Succession & DisabilityVP becomes president upon death or resignation. Provides a process for presidential incapacity. Tested in executive branch and presidential power questions.
26Voting Age: 18Can't deny the vote to citizens 18 or older. 1971. Passed during Vietnam — if old enough to be drafted, old enough to vote. Tested in political participation questions.
⚪ Low Priority — Know Briefly, Rarely Tested Directly
3No Quartering SoldiersGovernment can't force you to house soldiers. Rarely tested — mainly cited as historical grievance against Britain and as one piece of the privacy "penumbra" argument.
7Civil Jury TrialRight to jury in civil cases over $20. One of the only Bill of Rights protections NOT incorporated to states. Rarely appears on the AP exam.
11State Sovereign ImmunityStates can't be sued in federal court by citizens of another state. Background knowledge — not directly tested on the AP exam in most years.
12Electoral College FixSeparate ballots for president and VP. Important history (1800 Jefferson-Burr tie) but rarely tested directly. May come up in Electoral College context questions.
16Income TaxCongress can levy income taxes. Passed 1913. Occasionally appears in questions about the growth of federal power in the Progressive Era.
18 & 21Prohibition & Repeal18th banned alcohol (1919); 21st repealed it (1933). 21st is notable as the only amendment to repeal another. Low priority — may appear as historical example of the amendment process.
20Lame Duck / Inauguration DateChanged inauguration to January 20. "Lame duck" concept is tested, but the specific amendment rarely is.
23DC Electoral VotesDC gets 3 electoral votes. Very low priority for the exam.
27Congressional Pay DelayCongressional pay raises can't take effect until after the next election. Practically never tested on the AP exam.

FRQ 1 · Concept Application · 3 pts

A real-world political scenario is described. You apply course concepts to explain it.

Three sub-parts (A, B, C) — each worth 1 point. The scenario wraps around a concept you've studied. Your job: identify the concept, name it, and explain how it applies to that specific situation.

1
Read the scenario and ask: "What course topic is this really about?" A city council banning handguns → 2nd Amendment, McDonald v. Chicago. A president refusing to give Congress documents → executive privilege. A senator talking for hours to block a vote → filibuster.
2
Follow the task verb exactly. "Describe" = just state what the concept is. "Explain" = state it AND say why/how it works or what effect it has. "Draw a conclusion" = make a logical inference and explain your reasoning. These have different standards.
3
Name the concept in your first sentence. "This is an example of executive privilege" or "This demonstrates the iron triangle." Graders check boxes — give them the thing to check.
4
Always connect back to specific details in the scenario. Generic knowledge without referencing the scenario = 0 points. The scenario is the context you must apply your knowledge to.
Model answer structure: "Part A: This scenario illustrates [concept name]. [Concept] means [brief definition]. In this situation, [specific detail from scenario] shows [how concept applies]." Two to four sentences per part is enough.
FRQ 2 · Quantitative Analysis · 4 pts

You get a chart or graph. Read it accurately, explain patterns, connect to course concepts.

A visual (bar chart, pie chart, map, table) is provided. Sub-parts ask you to describe the data, identify trends, and explain them using what you've learned in class.

1
Read the title, labels, legend, and year range BEFORE answering anything. Students lose easy points by misreading what the chart is actually measuring. Take 30 seconds to understand the visual.
2
Use actual numbers. "Voter turnout was higher among older voters" = 0 pts. "Voters 65+ turned out at 72% compared to 38% for voters 18–29" = 1 pt. Specificity is what earns the point.
3
When explaining WHY a pattern exists, use a course concept. "This reflects the incumbency advantage — sitting members have more name recognition and easier fundraising." Connect data to ideas from class.
4
Extension questions need real analysis — not just restating the chart. If asked "how does this relate to political socialization?" — actually explain the relationship using your knowledge. The chart gave you data; your knowledge explains what it means.
Most common data topics: Voter turnout by age/race/income, public opinion polls, Electoral College maps, party ID trends over time, congressional approval ratings vs. incumbent reelection rates.
FRQ 3 · SCOTUS Comparison · 4 pts

A new, non-required case is described. You compare it to a required case you've memorized.

This question separates students who know their required cases from those who don't. A non-required case is given to you — your job is to find the required case it most resembles and analyze the comparison.

1
Identify the constitutional issue first. What right or clause is being argued about? 1st Amendment speech? Establishment Clause? Commerce Clause? Equal protection? That tells you which required case to reach for.
2
Name the required case and state its holding immediately. "This is similar to Tinker v. Des Moines (1969), in which the Supreme Court held that students retain 1st Amendment rights in public schools unless speech causes substantial disruption."
3
Similarity: share the constitutional principle, not just the topic. "Both cases raise the question of whether [constitutional right] can be restricted when [condition]. In both, the court applied the standard of [standard]." Topic overlap isn't enough — share the legal reasoning.
4
Difference: explain what legally distinguishes them. Different facts → different outcome. "Unlike Tinker, where the school had no evidence of disruption, here the speech occurred during a school-sponsored event, changing the school's legal relationship to the speech."
If you can't remember a case perfectly: name the constitutional provision, describe the principle the required case established, and reason from there. Partial credit exists for sound constitutional reasoning.
FRQ 4 · Argument Essay · 6 pts — The Most Important One

Take a clear position on a debatable claim and defend it with evidence from the course.

A debatable statement is given. You must argue FOR or AGAINST it using required documents, SCOTUS cases, and/or course concepts as evidence. This is 35% of all FRQ points.

Point Rubric
Defensible thesis1 pt
Contextualization1 pt
Evidence — describe a document/case1 pt
Evidence — use it to support thesis1 pt
Reasoning — explain the connection1 pt
Complexity — counterargument/nuance1 pt
Essay Structure (5 paragraphs)
¶1 Intro: Thesis — clear, specific, arguable position
¶2 Context: Historical background BEFORE the issue arose
¶3 Evidence 1: Name doc/case → explain it → connect to thesis
¶4 Evidence 2: Different evidence → same structure
¶5 Complexity: Counterargument + rebuttal, or a nuance
1
Thesis: pick a side and say WHY specifically. ✗ Bad: "There are pros and cons to the Electoral College." ✓ Good: "The Electoral College undermines democratic equality because it gives citizens in small states disproportionately more power per vote than citizens in large states." The thesis must be arguable — someone could disagree with it.
2
Contextualization means going BACK IN TIME — before the specific issue. If the prompt is about presidential power, start with the Constitutional Convention and the founders' fears of monarchy. Don't just restate the prompt. Explain the historical conditions that created the problem your thesis addresses.
3
Evidence: Name it → Explain what it says → Link it to your thesis. Don't just drop a case name. "In Federalist No. 51, Madison argued that 'ambition must counteract ambition' — that each branch needs tools to resist the others. This supports my thesis because [specific connection]." That's how you earn both evidence points.
4
Complexity: add one genuine layer of nuance. Options: (A) Raise the best counterargument and explain why your thesis still holds. (B) Explain where your argument has limits. (C) Compare across different time periods or groups. Must be substantive — one real sentence of nuance, not a throwaway "however, some disagree."
5
Use at least one required document AND one SCOTUS case for maximum evidence points. Different types of evidence (doc + case) strengthen the essay more than two of the same type.
Simple thesis formula: "Although [acknowledge the other side], [your clear position] because [reason 1] and [reason 2]." Example: "Although states have sometimes resisted federal authority, the national government has consistently expanded its power because of broad interpretations of the Commerce Clause and the Necessary and Proper Clause, as seen in McCulloch v. Maryland and the New Deal era."
★ Tips

How to Score Points — Practical Guide

Name the concept immediately. Graders use a scoring rubric — they're looking for specific things to check off. "This is the iron triangle" gives them the check. Describing it without naming it is risky. Vocabulary = points.
Write 2–4 focused sentences per sub-part (FRQs 1–3). Don't write a paragraph for a 1-point question. Get in, answer it, get out. Over-writing wastes time and earns no extra credit.
Always use specific numbers in data questions. "More older voters voted" = 0. "Voters 65+ turned out at 72% vs. 38% for 18–29-year-olds" = 1. The number is the point.
Write your thesis first, even if it's rough. If you run out of time, at minimum have a thesis. It's an automatic 1 point and you can't earn contextual or complexity points without it. Thesis goes in the intro paragraph only.
Know Federalist 10, 51, 70, 78, and Brutus 1 cold. They appear in the essay FRQ as potential evidence AND in SCOTUS comparison as required documents. Being able to cite Madison's argument specifically is worth real points.
Contextualization: go further back than the prompt suggests. If the prompt is about Brown v. Board, start at Reconstruction and the 14th Amendment — not "in 1954, segregation existed." Show the historical arc that led to the issue.
Never leave a sub-part blank. Every part is scored independently. A partial answer can still earn the point. Write something using the right vocabulary — you can't score what you don't write.
Don't confuse civil liberties and civil rights. Liberties = what government can't do to you (Bill of Rights). Rights = equal treatment under law (14th Amendment). Mixing them up signals a basic misunderstanding to the grader.
A "both sides" thesis earns zero. "The Electoral College has advantages and disadvantages" is not a thesis. Take a clear position. You won't lose points for being wrong — you lose points for not committing to an argument.
Complexity ≠ writing more. Adding another paragraph that repeats your argument doesn't earn the complexity point. One strong sentence that genuinely addresses a counterargument or limitation does. Quality, not quantity.
Multiple choice: eliminate clearly wrong answers first. Usually two answers are obviously off. Between the remaining two, the right answer is more specific and directly addresses the question. Watch for answers that are technically true but don't actually answer what was asked.
Time yourself: give FRQ 4 at least 30 minutes. It's worth 6 points — more than either FRQ 2 or 3. Students often under-invest in the essay. The other three FRQs split the remaining ~70 minutes.

Unit 1

Chemistry of Life

~8–11% of Exam
Water Properties
  • Cohesion — H-bonds hold water molecules together (surface tension)
  • Adhesion — water attracted to other polar surfaces (capillarity)
  • High specific heat — resists temperature changes; stabilizes climate
  • High heat of vaporization — evaporation cools surfaces
  • Polar solvent — dissolves ionic and polar compounds
  • Less dense as solid — ice floats; insulates aquatic ecosystems
Macromolecules Overview
  • Carbohydrates — C, H, O (1:2:1 ratio); energy, structure
  • Lipids — C, H, O; long-term energy, membranes, hormones
  • Proteins — C, H, O, N, S; enzymes, structure, signaling
  • Nucleic Acids — C, H, O, N, P; information storage (DNA/RNA)
  • All built by dehydration synthesis; broken by hydrolysis
Carbohydrates
  • Monomers = monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose)
  • Disaccharides joined by glycosidic linkages
  • Starch — α-glucose; energy storage in plants
  • Glycogen — α-glucose; energy storage in animals (liver/muscle)
  • Cellulose — β-glucose; structural (plant cell walls); indigestible
  • Chitin — β-glucose + N-acetyl; fungal walls, arthropod exoskeleton
Lipids
  • Triglycerides: 1 glycerol + 3 fatty acids (ester bonds)
  • Saturated FA — no double bonds; solid at room temp (animal fats)
  • Unsaturated FA — double bonds; liquid at room temp (plant oils)
  • Phospholipids — amphipathic; form bilayers; membrane structure
  • Steroids — 4 carbon rings; cholesterol, hormones (testosterone, estrogen)
  • Waxes — long-chain FA + alcohol; waterproofing
Proteins
  • Monomers = amino acids (20 types); linked by peptide bonds
  • 1° structure — AA sequence (encoded by DNA)
  • 2° structure — α-helix or β-pleated sheet (H-bonds along backbone)
  • 3° structure — overall 3D shape (R-group interactions)
  • 4° structure — multiple polypeptide subunits (e.g., hemoglobin)
  • Denatured by heat, pH change, heavy metals
Enzymes
  • Biological catalysts — lower activation energy; unchanged after reaction
  • Substrate binds at active site (induced fit model)
  • Cofactors (inorganic) & coenzymes (organic vitamins) assist
  • Competitive inhibition — inhibitor mimics substrate; blockable with more substrate
  • Noncompetitive inhibition — inhibitor changes shape of active site (allosteric site)
  • Feedback inhibition — end product inhibits enzyme earlier in pathway
Nucleic Acids

DNA — double helix; deoxyribose sugar; bases A, T, G, C; stores genetic info

RNA — single strand; ribose sugar; bases A, U, G, C; translates and carries genetic info

Nucleotide = 5-carbon sugar + phosphate group + nitrogenous base

Base pairing rules: A–T (2 H-bonds), G–C (3 H-bonds) in DNA; A–U in RNA

Purines: A, G (double ring)  |  Pyrimidines: T, C, U (single ring)

ATP = energy currency; ADP + Pᵢ ⇌ ATP

On the FRQ, always connect structure to function: e.g., explain WHY cellulose is structural (β-linkage creates straight, hydrogen-bondable fibers) vs. WHY starch is digestible (α-linkage is recognized by amylase).
02
Unit 2

Cell Structure & Function

~10–13% of Exam
Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes
  • Prokaryotes: no membrane-bound nucleus; circular DNA; ribosomes (70S); bacteria & archaea
  • Eukaryotes: membrane-bound nucleus; linear DNA; ribosomes (80S); animals, plants, fungi, protists
  • Both have: plasma membrane, ribosomes, DNA, cytoplasm
  • Endosymbiotic theory: mitochondria & chloroplasts were once free-living prokaryotes (own DNA, double membrane, 70S ribosomes)
Key Organelles
  • Nucleus — contains DNA; site of transcription; nuclear pores control traffic
  • Rough ER — ribosomes on surface; protein synthesis & modification
  • Smooth ER — lipid synthesis; detoxification; Ca²⁺ storage
  • Golgi — sorts, modifies, packages proteins; "post office"
  • Mitochondria — cellular respiration; ATP production; cristae increase SA
  • Lysosomes — hydrolytic enzymes; digestion; autophagy
  • Vacuoles — storage; central vacuole maintains turgor pressure (plants)
  • Chloroplasts — photosynthesis (plants/algae); thylakoids + stroma
Plasma Membrane
  • Fluid mosaic model — phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins
  • Hydrophilic heads face outward; hydrophobic tails face inward
  • Cholesterol — stabilizes fluidity (buffers against temp extremes)
  • Integral proteins — span the membrane (channels, transporters)
  • Peripheral proteins — on surface (signaling, support)
  • Glycoproteins/glycolipids — cell recognition (immune response)
Membrane Transport
  • Simple diffusion — small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) move with gradient; no energy
  • Facilitated diffusion — channel or carrier proteins; with gradient; no ATP
  • Osmosis — water moves toward higher solute concentration (semi-perm membrane)
  • Active transport — against gradient; requires ATP (Na⁺/K⁺ pump)
  • Endocytosis — phagocytosis (large), pinocytosis (fluids), receptor-mediated
  • Exocytosis — vesicle fuses with membrane; secretion
Tonicity & Osmosis
  • Hypertonic — more solute outside cell → cell shrinks (crenation in animals; plasmolysis in plants)
  • Hypotonic — less solute outside → cell swells; animal cells may lyse; plant cells become turgid (normal/healthy)
  • Isotonic — equal solute concentration; no net movement
  • Plant cells use turgor pressure for structural support
  • Water potential ψ = ψₛ + ψₚ (solute + pressure potential)
Cell Size & Surface Area
  • As cell volume ↑, SA:V ratio ↓ — limits efficient exchange
  • Smaller cells = higher SA:V ratio = more efficient transport
  • Adaptations to increase SA: microvilli (intestinal cells), cristae (mitochondria), thylakoid membranes
  • Cell specialization allows complex multicellular organisms
Water potential questions are common FRQs. Remember: water moves from high ψ to low ψ (i.e., from low solute to high solute). Adding solute lowers ψₛ, which lowers ψ overall.
03
Unit 3

Cellular Energetics

~12–16% of Exam
Thermodynamics
  • 1st Law — energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted
  • 2nd Law — every energy transfer increases entropy (disorder)
  • Exergonic rxn — releases free energy (ΔG < 0); spontaneous (e.g., cellular respiration)
  • Endergonic rxn — requires free energy (ΔG > 0); not spontaneous (e.g., photosynthesis)
  • ATP coupling — exergonic hydrolysis of ATP drives endergonic reactions
Photosynthesis Overview
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Light-dependent reactions — thylakoid membranes; produce ATP, NADPH, O₂
  • Calvin Cycle (light-independent) — stroma; uses ATP & NADPH to fix CO₂ into G3P → glucose
  • Photosystems PSII (P680) and PSI (P700) absorb light
  • Water splitting (photolysis) at PSII releases O₂ and electrons
  • Electron transport chain pumps H⁺ → ATP synthase → ATP (chemiosmosis)
Calvin Cycle (C3 plants)
  • Carbon fixation — RuBisCO fixes CO₂ to RuBP (5C) → 2× 3-PGA (3C)
  • Reduction — 3-PGA → G3P (uses ATP & NADPH)
  • Regeneration of RuBP — G3P → RuBP (uses ATP)
  • 3 CO₂ fixed per turn; 6 turns to produce 1 net G3P for export
  • Net: 3 CO₂ + 9 ATP + 6 NADPH → 1 G3P
Cellular Respiration
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ~30–32 ATP
  • Glycolysis — cytoplasm; glucose (6C) → 2 pyruvate (3C); net 2 ATP, 2 NADH
  • Pyruvate oxidation — mitochondrial matrix; pyruvate → acetyl-CoA + CO₂; 2 NADH
  • Krebs Cycle — matrix; 2 acetyl-CoA → 4 CO₂; 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 2 ATP
  • Oxidative phosphorylation — inner membrane; ETC + chemiosmosis → ~26–28 ATP
  • NADH and FADH₂ carry electrons to ETC; O₂ is final electron acceptor
Fermentation
  • Occurs when O₂ is absent (anaerobic); regenerates NAD⁺ to keep glycolysis running
  • Lactic acid fermentation — pyruvate + NADH → lactate + NAD⁺ (muscle cells, bacteria)
  • Alcohol fermentation — pyruvate → CO₂ + ethanol + NAD⁺ (yeast)
  • Net yield = only 2 ATP (from glycolysis alone)
C4 & CAM Plants
  • Photorespiration — RuBisCO binds O₂ instead of CO₂ when CO₂ is low; wasteful
  • C4 plants (corn, sugarcane) — pre-fix CO₂ as malate in mesophyll cells; transfer to bundle sheath for Calvin cycle; reduces photorespiration
  • CAM plants (cacti, succulents) — open stomata at night to fix CO₂ as malate; close stomata during day; adaptation to arid environments
Know where each stage of respiration occurs and what each produces. The AP exam frequently asks you to compare aerobic vs. anaerobic ATP yield and to explain chemiosmosis (proton gradient → ATP synthase).

Unit 4

Cell Communication & Cell Cycle

~10–15% of Exam
Types of Cell Signaling
  • Direct — gap junctions (animals) / plasmodesmata (plants); direct cytoplasm contact
  • Paracrine — local; signal diffuses to nearby cells (growth factors)
  • Endocrine — hormones travel via bloodstream to distant target cells
  • Autocrine — cell signals itself
  • Synaptic — neurotransmitters across synapse
Signal Transduction Pathway
  • Reception — ligand binds specific receptor (complementary shape)
  • Transduction — signal converted & amplified via relay molecules (cascade)
  • Response — gene expression change, enzyme activation, or other cellular response
  • G protein-coupled receptors (GPCR) — activate G protein → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP (second messenger)
  • Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTK) — dimerize upon ligand binding; phosphorylate intracellular proteins
Second Messengers
  • cAMP — produced by adenylyl cyclase; activates protein kinase A (PKA); amplifies signal
  • IP₃/DAG — from phospholipase C activation; IP₃ triggers Ca²⁺ release
  • Ca²⁺ — binds calmodulin; activates various kinases
  • Phosphorylation cascades amplify signals exponentially
  • Signal terminated by phosphodiesterase (degrades cAMP) or phosphatases
Cell Cycle Phases
  • G1 (Gap 1) — cell growth; protein synthesis; checkpoint: is cell large enough? DNA intact?
  • S (Synthesis) — DNA replication; each chromosome duplicated into 2 sister chromatids
  • G2 (Gap 2) — growth; prepare for division; checkpoint: DNA fully replicated?
  • M (Mitosis) — nuclear division (PMAT) + cytokinesis
  • G0 — non-dividing quiescent state (nerve, muscle cells)
Mitosis Stages
  • Prophase — chromatin condenses; spindle forms; nuclear envelope breaks down
  • Metaphase — chromosomes align at metaphase plate; kinetochore MTs attach
  • Anaphase — sister chromatids pulled to opposite poles by spindle fibers
  • Telophase — nuclear envelopes reform; chromosomes decondense
  • Cytokinesis — cleavage furrow (animals) or cell plate (plants) divides cytoplasm
Cell Cycle Regulation & Cancer
  • Cyclins + CDKs (cyclin-dependent kinases) — drive the cell cycle forward
  • Checkpoints — G1, G2, and spindle assembly checkpoint (M); monitor DNA integrity
  • Tumor suppressors — p53, Rb; slow or stop cell cycle; mutated in cancer
  • Proto-oncogenes → oncogenes when mutated; accelerate cell division
  • Apoptosis — programmed cell death; activated by cell damage or developmental signals; caspase cascade
Be ready to describe how a mutation in a proto-oncogene OR a tumor suppressor can lead to uncontrolled cell division. The exam loves asking: "Predict the effect on the cell cycle if protein X is mutated."

Unit 5

Heredity

~8–11% of Exam
Meiosis
  • Produces 4 haploid (n) gametes from one diploid (2n) parent cell
  • Meiosis I — homologous pairs separate (reductive division)
  • Meiosis II — sister chromatids separate (like mitosis)
  • Crossing over (prophase I) — homologs exchange segments at chiasmata → genetic recombination
  • Independent assortment (metaphase I) — random orientation of homolog pairs → 2ⁿ combinations
Mendelian Genetics
  • Law of Segregation — allele pairs separate during gamete formation
  • Law of Independent Assortment — genes on different chromosomes assort independently
  • Monohybrid: Aa × Aa → 3:1 phenotypic ratio; 1:2:1 genotypic ratio
  • Dihybrid: AaBb × AaBb → 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio
  • Testcross — unknown phenotype × homozygous recessive to determine genotype
Non-Mendelian Inheritance
  • Incomplete dominance — blended phenotype (red × white → pink); Rr is intermediate
  • Codominance — both alleles fully expressed (AB blood type); no blending
  • Multiple alleles — ABO blood type has 3 alleles (Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i)
  • Polygenic inheritance — multiple genes affect one trait (skin color, height); bell-curve distribution
  • Pleiotropy — one gene affects multiple traits (sickle cell affects many organs)
  • Epistasis — one gene masks expression of another (9:3:4, 12:3:1, etc.)
Sex-Linked Inheritance
  • X-linked traits — gene on X chromosome; males (XY) only have 1 copy → hemizygous
  • X-linked recessive (colorblindness, hemophilia): more common in males
  • Carrier females (Xᴬ Xᵃ) pass trait to 50% of sons
  • Barr bodies — inactivated X chromosomes in female cells (Lyon hypothesis)
  • Y-linked traits — passed only from father to all sons
Chromosomal Inheritance
  • Linked genes — on same chromosome; do NOT independently assort; tend to be inherited together
  • Recombination frequency — % recombinant offspring; used to map gene distance in centimorgans (cM)
  • Nondisjunction — failure of chromosomes to separate; produces aneuploid gametes
  • Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome); XXY (Klinefelter); XO (Turner)
  • Chromosomal mutations: deletion, duplication, inversion, translocation
Chi-Square Test
  • Used to determine if observed ratios differ significantly from expected ratios
  • χ² = Σ (O − E)² / E
  • Degrees of freedom = number of classes − 1
  • If χ² < critical value (p = 0.05) → fail to reject null hypothesis (data fits expected ratio)
  • If χ² > critical value → reject null (significant deviation)
Practice Punnett squares for dihybrid crosses AND chi-square calculations. Know how to interpret chi-square results and connect to the null hypothesis.

Unit 6

Gene Expression & Regulation

~12–16% of Exam
DNA Replication
  • Semiconservative — each new double helix has one old strand + one new strand
  • Helicase — unwinds and separates DNA strands at replication fork
  • Primase — synthesizes short RNA primer to initiate synthesis
  • DNA polymerase III — adds nucleotides 5′→3′; proofreads
  • Leading strand — synthesized continuously; Lagging strand — synthesized as Okazaki fragments
  • DNA ligase — joins Okazaki fragments; seals nicks
Transcription
  • DNA → RNA (in nucleus in eukaryotes)
  • RNA polymerase binds to promoter (TATA box in eukaryotes)
  • Template strand read 3′→5′; mRNA synthesized 5′→3′
  • Pre-mRNA processing (eukaryotes): 5′ cap added, poly-A tail added, introns removed, exons spliced together
  • Alternative splicing — same gene can produce different proteins
Translation
  • mRNA → protein (at ribosomes in cytoplasm)
  • Codons — 3-nucleotide sequences on mRNA; 64 total; 61 code for amino acids
  • AUG — start codon (Met); UAA, UAG, UGA — stop codons
  • tRNA — anticodon pairs with codon; carries specific amino acid
  • Ribosome A-P-E sites: A (incoming tRNA), P (peptidyl-tRNA), E (exit)
  • Peptide bond formed; polypeptide elongates until stop codon
Mutations
  • Point mutations — single base change
  • Silent — same amino acid (due to degeneracy of code); no effect
  • Missense — different amino acid; may alter protein function
  • Nonsense — premature stop codon; truncated, nonfunctional protein
  • Frameshift — insertion or deletion shifts reading frame; typically disruptive
  • Mutations in germ cells are heritable; somatic mutations are not
Gene Regulation in Prokaryotes
  • Operon — cluster of genes with shared promoter + operator (bacteria)
  • Lac operon — inducible; repressor normally blocks; lactose (allolactose) inactivates repressor → genes expressed
  • Trp operon — repressible; tryptophan (corepressor) activates repressor → genes blocked
  • Positive control — CAP-cAMP activates lac operon when glucose is low
Gene Regulation in Eukaryotes
  • Chromatin remodeling — histones modified; DNA accessibility changes
  • DNA methylation — adds CH₃ to cytosine; silences genes (epigenetic)
  • Histone acetylation — loosens chromatin → increased transcription
  • Transcription factors — bind enhancers/silencers; activate or repress RNA polymerase
  • miRNA/siRNA — small RNAs that silence gene expression post-transcriptionally (RNAi)
  • Epigenetics — heritable changes in gene expression without DNA sequence changes
Biotechnology

PCR — amplifies specific DNA; denaturation (94°C), annealing (50–60°C), extension (72°C); uses Taq polymerase

Gel electrophoresis — separates DNA/protein by size through agarose gel; smaller fragments migrate farther

CRISPR-Cas9 — guide RNA directs Cas9 nuclease to cut specific DNA sequence; enables gene editing

Restriction enzymes — cut DNA at specific palindromic sequences; produce sticky ends for cloning

Recombinant DNA — gene inserted into plasmid vector; transformed into bacteria for protein production (e.g., insulin)

DNA sequencing — Sanger method uses ddNTPs to terminate elongation; produces ladder of fragments

The central dogma (DNA → RNA → Protein) is foundational. Know the details of each step AND where regulation can occur: transcription initiation, mRNA processing, mRNA stability, translation, and post-translational modification.

Unit 7

Natural Selection & Evolution

~13–20% of Exam
Evidence for Evolution
  • Fossil record — documents changes in organisms over time; transitional fossils
  • Comparative anatomy — homologous structures (same origin, different function); vestigial structures
  • Analogous structures — similar function, different origin = convergent evolution
  • Molecular data — DNA/protein sequence similarity reflects common ancestry
  • Biogeography — species distribution patterns reflect evolutionary history
  • Embryology — similarities in early development among vertebrates
Natural Selection
  • Requires: heritable variation, differential survival/reproduction, selection pressure
  • Directional selection — shifts phenotype toward one extreme
  • Stabilizing selection — favors intermediate phenotype; reduces variation
  • Disruptive selection — favors extremes over intermediates; increases variation
  • Sexual selection — traits increase mating success (intrasexual vs. intersexual)
  • Acts on phenotype; changes allele frequencies in population
Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
p + q = 1   |   p² + 2pq + q² = 1
  • p = dominant allele freq; q = recessive allele freq
  • p² = homozygous dominant; 2pq = heterozygous; q² = homozygous recessive
  • Conditions for HWE: large population, random mating, no mutation, no migration, no natural selection
  • Useful null hypothesis to test if evolution is occurring
Mechanisms of Evolution
  • Mutation — ultimate source of new alleles; random; often neutral
  • Gene flow — movement of alleles between populations; reduces differences
  • Genetic drift — random allele frequency changes; stronger in small populations
  • Bottleneck effect — drastic reduction in population size; loss of variation
  • Founder effect — small group colonizes new area; limited genetic variation
  • Natural selection — nonrandom; acts on existing variation
Speciation
  • Biological species concept — species = group that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring
  • Allopatric speciation — geographic isolation leads to reproductive isolation; most common
  • Sympatric speciation — speciation without geographic barrier (polyploidy in plants, niche differentiation)
  • Reproductive isolating mechanisms: prezygotic (behavioral, temporal, mechanical, gametic, habitat) and postzygotic (hybrid sterility, hybrid inviability)
  • Polyploidy — extra sets of chromosomes; instant speciation in plants
Phylogenetics & Cladistics
  • Cladogram / phylogenetic tree — shows evolutionary relationships based on shared derived characters
  • Clade (monophyletic group) — ancestor + ALL descendants
  • Synapomorphies — shared derived characters; define clades
  • Parsimony — simplest tree (fewest evolutionary changes) preferred
  • Molecular clocks — mutation rates used to estimate divergence times
  • 3 domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya
Hardy-Weinberg calculations are almost always on the AP exam. Practice: given q² (homozygous recessive frequency), find q, then p, then 2pq (carrier frequency). Also know how to interpret and draw cladograms.
08
Unit 8

Ecology

~10–15% of Exam
Levels of Ecological Organization
  • Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biome → Biosphere
  • Population — same species in one area; density, distribution, age structure
  • Community — all species in an area; interactions, diversity
  • Ecosystem — community + abiotic factors; energy flow, nutrient cycles
  • Abiotic factors: temperature, light, water, nutrients, pH, salinity
Population Ecology
  • Exponential growth — unlimited resources; J-shaped curve; dN/dt = rN
  • Logistic growth — limited resources; S-shaped curve; dN/dt = rN(K−N/K)
  • Carrying capacity (K) — max sustainable population size in an environment
  • r-selected species — many small offspring, little parental care, unstable environments
  • K-selected species — few offspring, high parental care, stable environments
  • Age structure diagrams — predict future population growth
Survivorship Curves
  • Type I — low early mortality, most die old (humans, large mammals)
  • Type II — constant mortality rate throughout life (birds, rodents)
  • Type III — high early mortality; survivors live long (fish, trees, insects)
  • Reflects life history strategy and parental investment
Species Interactions
  • Predation (+/−) — drives coevolution; prey adaptations (crypsis, mimicry, warning coloration)
  • Competition (−/−) — interspecific: between species; intraspecific: within species
  • Competitive exclusion — two species cannot occupy same niche indefinitely
  • Character displacement — species diverge in sympatry to reduce competition
  • Mutualism (+/+) — mycorrhizae, nitrogen-fixing bacteria, cleaner fish
  • Commensalism (+/0) — barnacles on whales, epiphytes
  • Parasitism (+/−) — parasite benefits, host harmed
Community Ecology
  • Keystone species — disproportionate effect on community; removal causes trophic cascade
  • Ecological succession: Primary (bare rock → mature forest); Secondary (disturbed area with soil)
  • Pioneer species — first to colonize; lichens, mosses
  • Climax community — stable end stage of succession
  • Biodiversity — species richness + evenness; increases ecosystem stability and resilience
Energy Flow in Ecosystems
  • Trophic levels: producers → primary consumers → secondary → tertiary
  • 10% rule — only ~10% of energy transferred between trophic levels (rest lost as heat)
  • Gross primary productivity (GPP) — total photosynthesis
  • Net primary productivity (NPP) = GPP − respiration
  • Food webs show complex energy pathways; more links = greater stability
  • Decomposers (bacteria/fungi) recycle nutrients from dead organic matter
Biogeochemical Cycles
  • Carbon cycle — photosynthesis (fixes CO₂), respiration (releases CO₂), decomposition, combustion
  • Nitrogen cycle — N₂ fixation (Rhizobium), nitrification, assimilation, denitrification, ammonification
  • Phosphorus cycle — no gaseous phase; weathering of rocks; phosphate taken up by plants
  • Water cycle — evaporation, transpiration, precipitation, runoff, groundwater
  • Eutrophication — excess N & P → algal blooms → O₂ depletion → dead zones
Global Change & Conservation
  • Climate change — rising CO₂ from fossil fuels; greenhouse effect; ocean acidification
  • Habitat fragmentation — reduces biodiversity; increases extinction risk (island biogeography)
  • Invasive species — outcompete natives; lack natural predators
  • Biological magnification — toxin concentration increases up food chain (DDT, mercury)
  • Island biogeography — species richness ∝ island size and proximity to mainland
Know how to calculate NPP and interpret energy flow diagrams. Ecological hierarchy questions often appear as data analysis: be ready to explain population growth curves, survivorship graphs, and food web diagrams using evidence.
